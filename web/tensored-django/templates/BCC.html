Overview

Basal cell carcinoma (BCC) is a prevalent form of skin cancer that originates in the basal cells, the deepest layer of the epidermis. This slow-growing cancer typically manifests as abnormal, uncontrolled growths, often presenting as pearly nodules, translucent bumps, or open sores. Although BCC rarely metastasizes to other organs, its local invasiveness can lead to tissue destruction if not addressed in a timely manner.

Causes and Risk Factors

  • Excessive sun exposure
  • Sunburns
  • Fair skin
  • Genetic predisposition
  • Exposure to ionizing radiatsion
  • Exposure to certain environmental toxins

Symptoms

Recognizing BCC involves identifying persistent changes in the skin, such as the appearance of new growths or alterations in existing moles. Common symptoms include the development of pearly or waxy nodules, non-healing sores, or scar-like lesions.

Treatment

Treatment modalities for BCC encompass surgical excision, Mohs micrographic surgery for precise removal, cryotherapy to freeze cancer cells, and topical medications. The choice of treatment depends on factors like the tumor's size, depth, and location.

Prevention

Preventive measures against BCC involve diligent sun protection, including the use of sunscreen and protective clothing. Regular self-examinations and professional skin checks contribute to early detection and intervention.

This overview provides a glimpse into the pathology of BCC. For a comprehensive understanding of your specific situation, consult with a healthcare professional.
